# Bias Control Rules

## Purpose

Use this guide to reduce analytical distortion when researching unreleased games through public information, comments, reviews, and creator videos.

Bias cannot be fully removed.
Bias must be surfaced, labeled, and prevented from silently turning into conclusions.

## Core Rule

Every conclusion must answer:

1. What is the source type?
2. What kind of bias is likely present?
3. What part is observed fact?
4. What part is interpretation?
5. How representative is this signal?

If these are unclear, confidence must be lowered.

## Main Bias Categories

### 1. Official Messaging Bias

Common in press releases, reveal trailers, official interviews, feature posts, and publisher copy.

Control rules:

- use official sources for dates, names, announced features, and stated direction
- do not use official language alone to prove quality, depth, polish, or user satisfaction
- mark aspirational statements as `stated intent`, not `confirmed delivery`

### 2. Media Recap / PR Relay Bias

Common in news rewrites, launch-window previews, SEO summaries, and low-originality recap articles.

Control rules:

- trace back to the earliest source
- do not treat repeated rewrites as independent confirmation
- downgrade confidence if most articles cite the same announcement

### 3. Fandom Bias

Common in official PV comments, IP-heavy communities, franchise pages, and launch-hype threads.

Control rules:

- separate `IP attachment` from `gameplay evaluation`
- do not treat fandom enthusiasm as broad market validation
- preserve when fans and genre-core players disagree

### 4. Experience-Selection Bias

Common in long reviews from testers, deep forum posts, and creator feedback from limited test periods.

Control rules:

- label likely reviewer type
- distinguish `firsthand_experience` from `expectation` and `projection`
- do not generalize niche-core feedback to future mass audience response without caution

### 5. Creator Incentive Bias

Common in sponsored preview videos, creator access programs, hype-cycle content, and trend-chasing channels.

Control rules:

- record sponsorship/access signals
- separate `creator claim` from `footage-supported observation`
- cross-check creator points with Section 2 and Section 3 evidence

### 6. Sampling Bias

Common in top-comment-only analysis, recent-comment-only analysis, one-platform-only analysis, and partial scraping.

Control rules:

- record capture method explicitly
- preserve whether data is full, partial, or sampled
- avoid direct comparison of raw counts across platforms
- prefer ratio, topic structure, and quote evidence over raw volume

### 7. Translation / Language Bias

Common in bilingual analysis, slang-heavy comments, sarcasm, jargon, and machine-translated transcripts.

Control rules:

- preserve original quote whenever possible
- store translated text separately
- lower confidence if meaning depends on nuanced phrasing
- avoid over-reading exact emotional intensity across languages

### 8. Analyst Bias

Common in overfitting to prior beliefs, forcing a clean thesis, and preferring elegant narratives over mixed evidence.

Control rules:

- preserve disagreement and unresolved points
- separate `What We Know` from `What We Infer`
- explicitly record open questions
- if evidence is mixed, the report must remain mixed

## Cross-Section Bias Checks

Before final synthesis, compare all sections.

### Check 1: Official Appeal vs Hands-On Reality

If Section 2 is strongly positive but Section 3 is cautious or negative, interpret as strong surface appeal but unproven deeper systems.

### Check 2: Creator Narrative vs Player Experience

If Section 4 is positive but Section 3 shows repeated firsthand concerns, keep both.

### Check 3: News Coverage vs Evidence Quality

If Section 1 has many articles but all come from one reveal cycle, reduce confidence.

### Check 4: Cross-Market Tone Differences

If CN and global reactions differ, preserve region-specific expectation structures.

## Mandatory Labels in Findings

Use these labels where appropriate:

- `strong factual basis`
- `credible but limited`
- `signal exists, representativeness uncertain`
- `likely promotional framing`
- `firsthand but not necessarily representative`
- `creator interpretation, partially supported`
- `translation may affect nuance`
- `mixed evidence, keep open`

## Escalation Triggers

Escalate or lower confidence when:

- most evidence comes from one source family
- only sampled comments are available
- most strong claims depend on translation
- creator claims cannot be grounded in footage
- review identity is highly uncertain
- official materials dominate the evidence base

## Forbidden Moves

- Do not convert repetition into confirmation.
- Do not convert confidence of tone into confidence of evidence.
- Do not erase disagreement for narrative cleanliness.
- Do not merge different source types into one undifferentiated sentiment score.
- Do not present high-engagement comments as representative without caveat.
